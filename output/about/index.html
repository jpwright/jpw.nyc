In more detail

I was born and raised in Peekskill, NY, although I also lived in the nearby hamlets of Cortlandt Manor and Yorktown Heights. After a childhood mostly filled with discovering the forest outside my house, injuring myself in creative ways, and Game Boy Color, I found my first true love in high school: policy debate. I spent many weekends traveling to tournaments across the Northeast, learning to research, think, argue, and speak. I spent summers at the University of Michigan and at the University of Kentucky training and practicing with other students from across the country. After a senior campaign that culminated in a New York state championship and a top 10 finish in the national Baker Award standings, I went off to college.

At Cornell University I decided to study engineering, and quickly found a passion for tinkering with electronics and computers. I built my first circuit in a freshman optics class: a 555 timer circuit to control pulse generation for a Nd:YAG laser. I wrote my first (serious) programs: image processing scripts in MATLAB for a robotics class. I played clarinet in Cornell's wind ensemble, I didn't make the cut for the ultimate frisbee team, I helped out on some robotics projects in the information science department. I kept the debating up, too: at the end of my senior year, I was a two-time qualifier for the National Debate Tournament, and had a "sweet 16" finish in the CEDA National Championship. I was honored to receive the Venephe A. Wilson award from the Cornell Forensics Society for service to the program.

I stayed at Cornell for my Master's degree in electrical and computer engineering, where I had the opportunity to specialize more and gain more practical experience. I started to develop a focus in optoelectronics: my Master's thesis involved designing and building a light field camera, and I took classes in fiber optics, waveguides, optical computing, and nonlinear optics.

I spent my summers working at various technology-related internships, but none stood out as much as my summer with hackNY, a non-profit organization that pairs technical students with startups in NYC. Beyond an awesome internship with Next Jump and a dorm at NYU, I had an amazing class of fellows from across the U.S. and Canada and had the privilege of meeting some really inspirational people from the NY tech scene.

This year I'm moving to Santa Cruz, CA to begin life in the real world, not knowing quite what to expect but optimistic about the future. I'm guided by a desire to change the world: by improving people's lives, by fixing something that's broken, by contributing to humanity's understanding of the natural universe.
